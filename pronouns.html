Pronouns

Personal pronouns are a fruitful nuisance in this language and should have been left out.



If you'd prefer to skip straight to the tables, click here to go to the singular pronoun table or here for the plural pronoun table.





Singular

Me

Let’s start way at the beginning. Everyone loves talking about themselves, right? Well, now we’re going to learn how. Crack your knuckles and get to it.

  • ich - this is the nominative form of 'I' and the concrete foundation of everyone’s favourite personal pronoun: everything that follows will build on it.
  • mich - 'for me.' What case is this? Try to remember - go on, guess for a moment!

    ...Okay, it's accusative.

  • mir – ‘to me.’ Dative.
  • mein - 'my.' This is used in the genitive case to show possession of something and changes depending on the gender of the thing possessed (for example, if someone owns a cat (die Katze), then they would say meine Katze to show that it is their cat. Note the way the ending of the word changes from 'mein' to 'meine' - this is an easy way to keep track of gender in German.)


You

  • du - nominative form of 'You.'
  • dich - 'for you.' Accusative.
  • dir - 'to you.' Dative.
  • dein - 'yours.' Genitive.


Third person

In German, the third person functions much the same as it does in English. You use the pronuns 'he,' 'she' and 'it' and the verbs between them don't change - eg. 'have' is the same whether you're saying er habe or sie habe or es habe. The only main difference is in the dative and accusative cases - in German, there is a different word for 'her' depending on whether you are saying 'to her' or 'for her,' and the same goes for the other two pronouns.



He

  • er - 'he.' Nominative case.
  • ihn - 'for him.' Accusative case.
  • ihm - 'to him.' Dative case.
  • Sein - 'his.' Genitive case.


She

  • sie - 'she.' Nominative case.
  • sie - 'for her.' Accusative case. It looks the same as the nominative case, so don't get them confused! 'Sie' and 'für sie' are deceptively similar.
  • ihr - 'to her.' Dative case.
  • ihr - 'hers/her [thing].' This changes depending on the gender of the thing you're talking about her owning, so it shouldn't be confused with the dative - also, context helps!


It

  • es - 'it.' Nominative case.
  • es - ' for it.' Accusative case. Again, the accusative and nominative forms look very similar but context helps!
  • ihm - 'to it.' Dative case.
  • sein - 'its.' Genitive case. Don't confuse this with 'it's' - that would be 'es ist.'

Plural

Now we're getting into the group work! If you're out with two or more people, now you know how to boast about it.



Wir

  • wir - nominative form of 'us.'
  • uns - 'for us.' Accusative.
  • uns - 'to us.' Dative.
  • unser - 'ours.' Genitive.


Ihr (you plural)

  • ihr - nominative form of 'you.'
  • euch - 'for you.' Accusative.
  • euch - 'to you.' Dative.
  • eure - 'yours.' Genitive.


Sie

Sie is used in third person to mean both 'they' and the polite form of 'you.' There is no difference in how it is declined, except for the fact that 'polite' Sie uses capital letters. See the table for more.

  • sie - Nominative.
  • sie - Accusative.
  • ihnen - Dative.
  • ihr - Genitive.
