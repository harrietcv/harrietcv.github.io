Cases

We need to get a few things straight. Find your nearest sheet of paper, phone, wall or hieroglyphic tablet and make some notes, because these are important things we need to keep in mind from this point onwards.

German is a language that relies on four cases to form sentences. A case helps us explain what is happening in a sentence by changing what happens to the words. For example, things can happen to, for or with someone – these are all three different cases.

We don’t really have an easy equivalent in English for some of them: English has three and largely lost the fourth (we don’t really have a dative case anymore) while German has a nominative, accusative, dative and genitive case.

What does any of that even mean? Time for a grammar lesson!



Nominative

The nominative case is the subject of the verb in a sentence – they are performing whatever action the verb describes. For example, ‘I walk’ is in the nominative case: nobody is walking to that person, and there is nothing else happening in the sentence. Just some lonely person wandering around, poor fellow.



Accusative

The accusative case is the direct object of the verb in the sentence – they are having whatever the verb describes done to them. For example, ‘I walk to her’ contains both the nominative and accusative cases: ‘I’ is the person doing the walking (nominative) and ‘she’ is the person being walked towards (accusative) – in other words, the walking is being ‘done to her’ by the other person. Our person made a friend!



Dative

The dative case is the indirect object of the verb in the sentence – they are having something given to them. For example, ‘I give a book to her’ has nominative, accusative and dative cases – ‘she’ (dative) is being given something (accusative – in this case, a book) by a person (I, nominative). Our person is trying to become even better friends!



Genitive

The genitive case indicates who owns something in a sentence. For example, ‘I give the man’s book to her’ has the nominative, accusative, dative and genitive cases all in the same sentence – I (nominative) am giving her (dative) a book (accusative) that belongs to a man (genitive – you can imagine it being ‘the book of the man’ to simplify things). Our person stole somebody’s book to deepen their friendship with her! Isn’t that sweet?



Now what does that whole thing look like in German?

Ich gebe (I give/am giving) ihr (to her) das Buch (the book) des Mannes (of the man).

Earlier I mentioned direct and indirect objects. There'll be more on that here, but for now I'll just leave you with this so that it makes a bit more sense: German is a language that follows the subject-verb-object pattern, or SVO for short. Depending on the verb, there may be an object in the sentence, and what object it is also depends on the verb.





Prepositions

Just like how we say 'for' or 'to' something and so on in English, German has a whole collection of things called prepositions. They don't really mean anything on their own, but they help make the sentence clearer.

In German, each preposition has a specific case, which is why I figured it made more sense just to stick this at the end here so you can scroll back to the explanations above if things get tricky. Some can be used with more than one, while some just choose a case and stick to it; not all of them have objects, either.

Being placed with specific verbs can also change their meaning, too. Hey, nobody said German was easy, did they?

Having a table where you can see things clearly laid out would probably help, right? Well, here you go! There are many prepositions in German, just as there are in English, so there isn't space to really list all of them, but I wanted to give you a little flavour of the sorts of things you might come across or use yourself.

NB: if you see a verb that has 'sich' as part of it, that means you have to correspond it to the appropriate pronoun when speaking. For example, 'ask after something' is sich erkundigen nach etwas, but to say 'I ask after something,' this would be ich erkundige mich nach etwas. This is what is known as a 'reflexive verb' - more on verbs here!
