Word Order

The order of a (typical) German sentence is SVO, or subject-verb-object. The subject is the person ‘performing’ the verb while the object is the person having the verb ‘done to them.’

Here's an example: the dog eats the cactus.

Der Hund isst den Kaktus.

However, as with most things in learning a language, there are exceptions to this rule. So long as the verb comes second place in the sentence (and yes, there are exceptions for that, too), you could make it an OVS sentence, or object-verb-subject.

From the example above: the cactus is eaten [by] the dog.

Den Kaktus isst der Hund.

We know the cactus is not eating the dog because of the endings of the words in the sentence. Den is accusative, making the cactus the object, while der is nominative, so the dog is the subject. (Need a refresher on cases? Click here!)

Objects

You may have seen me going on about things like direct objects on other parts of the website and not have a clue what I'm talking about. Well, it'll all make sense soon! (...Hopefully.)

In a nutshell, 'objects' are ways of describing how the verb affects the sentence. You were probably told in primary school about how your actions affect other people, and this is no different - the central 'action' here has consequences. It'll (hopefully) make more sense if we look at some examples in context below.



Direct object

The direct object is directly affected by the verb, so something is being 'done' to them. In the little sentences 'I fed the dogs', ‘the dogs’ are the direct objects because they are being fed).



Indirect object

The indirect object is not directly affected by the verb, but it describes how other things are. It's generally used where something is being given or taken (so dative case, if you're keeping you're eyes peeled!)

In the sentence 'I give him a flower', ‘he’ is not having anything done to him (although being given free flowers is always nice), but the flower is have something 'done' to it, because it is ‘being given’.

In English, we would say something like "I give money to Cara," and that is immediately understood, but in German you don't use a preposition like 'to' for the same sentence: you would just say "I give Cara money," and the endings of the words in the sentence would make their respective cases clear enough.



Prepositional object

Prepositions in German function more or less the same way they do in English, and with similarly arbitrary associates. For example, in English you would say 'I'm looking forward to [something.]' In German, this would be 'ich freue mich auf [etwas].' Some prepositions have their own objects, such as 'an' (accusative object). More about prepositions here!



Getting trickier...

Let's talk about clauses.

In German, main clauses function the way we've seen above: verb goes to second place. But if you use a subordinate clause, the verb goes to the end of the sentence.

Because subordinate clauses depend on the main clause to make sense, you might see them sometimes referred to as 'dependent clauses.' Think of it this way: a main clause is a sentence that works by itself (such as 'Joe went to the shops') and a subordinate clause adds extra information to the sentence for a little sparkle (eg. 'Joe went to the shops because he was hungry.')

We don't generally think of things like clauses and word order in English (at least, not if you're a native speaker) because that's not how we're taught the language growing up, so trying to learn about all the rules other languages have when we're not entirely familiar with our own (and often break them anyway) can be a little confusing and, at times, stressful.
