Verbs



Principal parts

Every German verb has three major forms, or principal parts. These are useful because if we are familiar with them then we can construct the forms for all other tenses, the passive and subjunctive.

The principal parts are:

  • The infinitive - this is the form you will find verbs listed as in the dictionary; also, the present tense and Subjunctive I are based on it. You also use it to form both the future tense and conditional. (An example is machen)
  • The past tense in the first person singular form - Subjunctive II is based on this. (From the above example, an example is machte)
  • The past participle - this is used to make the compound perfect tenses and the passive. (from the above example, an example is gemacht)


A good way of thinking about it is that regular (ie. most) verbs will take the -te ending in past tense from their root form (eg. the root of 'machen' is 'mach - (en) = mach,' so you just have to add -te at the end) and then ge____t in their past participle form (eg. gemacht.)

Irregular verbs have a vowel change in their past tense form and change to ge-(vowel change)-en in their past participle form.

Sadly, as with a few things in German, there is no shortcut to learning irregular verbs and their past/future forms - you just have to sit down and consciously memorise them all.



Endings

In the present tense form, these are the endings verbs will take:

Pronoun Ending
ich -e
du -st
er/sie/es -t
wir -en
ihr -t
sie/Sie -en


The endings can vary depending on which tense you use, but that's going to take a much longer and more confusing explanation. Speaking of tenses...



Tenses

There are six tenses in German, each corresponding to a different timeframe. They function in a relatively similar way to English and (in most cases) are used the same way.



Tense Example Meaning
Present ich kaufe I buy
Past ich kaufte I bought
Perfect ich habe gekauft I have bought
Pluperfect ich hatte gekauft I had bought
Future ich werde kaufen I will/shall buy
Future Perfect ich werde gekauft haben I will/shall have bought

Separable and inseparable verbs

Inseparable verbs

Inseparable verbs, as the name suggests, cannot be separated. What you see is what you get: the prefix is always attached to the verb. They also don't add ge- in the past participle form.

Here's an example:



Infinitive Past Tense Past participle
bestellen bestellte bestellt


Separable verbs

Remember what I said about - you can do that here, too! In its past and present forms, you just have to cut off the prefix (eg 'auf' from 'aufmachen') and send it hurtling to the end! In all other cases, the prefix stays attached, but in its past participle form the 'ge' goes in between the prefix and the rest of the verb (eg weglaufen + ge = weggelaufen). Separable verbs are freeing to use!
