Determiners

Determiners in German function the same way they do in English - they precede the noun and make it make sense. This can be an article, demonstrative or possessive.

Now we're going to make some sense out of all that word soup.



Articles

Articles in German work the same way 'a' or 'the' do in English and are split into two separate but similar roles: definite and indefinite.



Definite article

Definite articles correspond to the word 'the' in English and precede a noun to show its number (whether it is a singular or plural word), its case and its gender.



  masculine feminine neuter plural
nominative der die das die
accusative den die das die
genitive des der des der
dative dem der dem den


You can also find the definitive article commonly combined with prepositions into a single word. The ones below are the ones you will encounter and use most often and are easily understood, although it is still grammatically possible and correct to use them separately - it is only joined for convenience and brevity rather than a hard and fast 'rule.'



Combination Result
an + das ans
an + dem am
bei + dem beim
in + das ins
in + dem im
von + dem vom
zu + dem zum
zu + der zur


Indefinite article

The indefinite article corresponds to 'a/an' in English and reflects the noun's gender and case. There is no plural form, same as English, because indefinite plural nouns do not need an article. As an example, you would say (in both English and German), 'I bought an egg,' but it would not make sense in either language to say 'I have bought a eggs' or 'I have brought an eggs.' You would just say, 'I have bought eggs.' One less thing to have to keep track of!



  masculine feminine neuter
nominative ein eine ein
accusative einen eine ein
dative einem einer einem
genitive eines einer eines


German also has a negative form of the indefinite article: kein. This corresponds in English to 'not a [thing],' 'not any [thing] (when discussing something in plural form)' or 'no [thing]'. The endings all corespond to the ons above, so you don't have to learn anything extra on top, but it can also be used with singular words, unlike 'ein'.

NB:You do not say 'nicht ein,' always the appropriate form of 'kein.'

Here are some examples:

  • Ich habe keinen Hund - I don't have a dog
  • Du hast keine Telefonladegeräte, oder? - You don't have any phone chargers, do you?
  • Es gab keine Deutschen in der Nähe, die übersetzen werden konnten. - There were no Germans nearby who could translate.

Demonstratives

Demonstratives function in German like saying 'this' or 'that.' Imagine you're pointing out something or somebody to someone else and then you get the idea.



der/die/das

There's no change in the way these are used (same written form as the definite article, after all), but there's more emphasis. As an example, imagine you've found suddenly something that you were frantically searching for and had given up hope of ever seeing again this side of the 22nd century. In the blissful bubble of your little eureka moment, you're not just going to say, "Ah! This was what I was looking for!" in the same tone you would discuss a pair of old socks or a rerun of [in the interest of impartiality, insert your least enjoyed film/TV show here], are you? No, there's going to be a very audible difference. You're going to triumphantly wave your fist in the air while yelling, "This was what I was looking for!"

It's the same principle in German, really. To get the full experience, you should try saying these phrases out loud and really emphasise the words. I won't even stress the words while typing because I'm leaving it in your hands to put the life back in these sentences. Ham it up, I dare you. Do it for Mark Twain's enduring disapproval:

  • Schau! Die ist eine wirklich interessante Website! - Look! This is a really interesting website!
  • Warum hast du das Kleid gekauft? - why did you buy that dress?
  • Der Hund ärgert mich - that dog annoys me


dieser

Much like the above, dieser is like saying 'this' in German, only more formal. It's typically used to avoid ambiguity (such as in written German, but it features in spoken German, too). The different endings specify the gender, case and number of the noun specified.



  masculine feminine neuter plural
nominative dieser diese dieses diese
accusative diesen diese dieses diese
dative diesem dieser diesem diesen
genitive dieses dieser diesem dieser


Possessives

You've already seen possessives in the pronouns section - 'mein', 'dein' and so on. The possessive determiner does exactly the same thing we've already seen a few times now - it says how many things you own, and what gender they are. There's a different form of 'my', for example, for talking about feminine nouns than there is for masculine, and so on and so forth.

They have the same endings as 'ein' and 'kein,' but I'll put it into a table for handy visual reference anyway.

Let's see that in action! You're becoming a pro at this already:



  masculine feminine neuter plural
nominative mein meine meines meine
accusative meinen meine meines meine
dative meinem meiner meinem meinen
genitive meines meiner meinem meiner
