Pronunciation

Learning a language is always tricky but learning how to pronounce things properly is probably one of the hardest things people face when speaking more than one language, especially if the other language has sounds not encountered in their native one.

German isn’t entirely different from English (it uses the same alphabet for a start), but it does have four letters that we don’t have. They’re not impossible to replicate; it just might take a little practice and getting used to.



Umlauts and the Eszett

These are the three letters you’ve probably seen a few times and have the little dots over the top. In German, those dots are called the umlaut. The corresponding letters (Ä, Ö and Ü) can be referred to as ‘A umlaut,’ ‘O umlaut’ and ‘U umlaut’ when spoken about to distinguish them from their regular forms. Sometimes they might be printed as “ae,” “oe” and “ue,” particularly in old German names (such as Goethe) or for those using a keyboard without the letters or in places where the text may not display properly.

NB: do NOT confuse these with the letters ‘ï’ or ‘ë’ – though they look like the umlaut letters below, these are not found in the German language.

For Ö and Ü you want to pucker your lips - make sure your tongue is at the front of your mouth. Practising in the mirror might be useful and is also a perfect way to escape doing other, less amusing tasks.

  • Ä / ä - 'eh,' like the e in egg. (eg: Äpfel)
  • Ö / ö - sort of like 'err,' like in 'gurney' (if you blend out the r at the end): this one doesn’t have so many easy equivalents I can think of in English, so the video below will probably help better. (eg: Österreich)
  • Ü / ü - like ‘ooh’ but more puckered and closer to ‘ouh’; depending on how you pronounce fondue, it may sound like the ending ‘ooh’. (eg: über)




Whether you call it the eszett or the scharfes S, ß is simply another way of writing ‘ss’ in nouns. There are a whole host of official rules around how and when to use it, but luckily it’s not so difficult to pronounce – but don’t get it confused with a capital B! The alphabet

The German alphabet isn't pronounced too differently from how we would pronounce it in English: the main difference I've found is the stereotypical 'harshness' in how German is pronounced phonetically because stresses and emphases are different. Still, if a native German speaker was trying to spell out their name to you, you'd probably get the general idea after a while.

This is a table that tells you how each letter is pronounced phonetically - it's a useful starting point to figuring out how the language works. For example, looking at the 'yot' of J and the corresponding letters, you can work out that Junge would be pronounced 'yoong-uh.' Magic!

Vowel combinations

In German, whenever these vowels are paired together, they are always pronounced as follows:

  • ai or ei - 'eye' (eg. bei, Mai)
  • au - 'ow' (eg. auch)
  • eu or äu - 'oi' (eg. Häser, eure)
  • ie - 'ee' (eg. niedrig)

As a tip, check the vowel order when you're dealing with 'ie' and 'ei' - Biene and Beine mean very different things!



Tricky sounds

These sounds don't come so intuitively for non-native speakers and can sometimes be a little frustrating. Once you've got to grips with them, though, the good news is that you're pretty much sorted! German isn't so 'lawless' as the English language is and it's not so free about breaking its own rules, so pronunciation is generally pretty consistent.



Pronouncing "ch"

The "ch" sound in German is pronounced like the "ch" in 'loch' when it comes after a, u, o or au.

Here are some examples:

  • das Buch
  • auch
  • der Ochse
  • der Geruch



If it doesn't come after one of those sounds, then the "ch" becomes much more softer and gentle, almost like an 'h' in English but with slightly more friction. It's difficult to really describe properly - you have to just try it out for yourself, but don't try stressing over it too much! German is really not worth getting an aneurysm over, I assure you.

  • die Milch
  • das Mädchen
  • mich
  • welche



Pronouncing "pf"

This is a sound that we don't really have or use in the English language - you have to pronounce both the 'p' and 'f' sounds individually but also at the same time, so it sounds like 'puh-fuh' (but very quickly). You need to make that happen in the space of saying a single letter, after all! A good tip is to think of a word with an 'f' sound, like 'fire', then add a 'puh' sound before it quickly. This one will take some practice.

  • das Pferd
  • der Pfennig



Pronouncing "qu"

Don't get misled by the letters - these in combination are actually pronounced 'kv.' Similar to the above, you're fitting two sounds into the space of one letter with all the stress on the second sound: here, 'kuh-vuh' becomes 'k'VUH' when you say it at the speed of normal conversation.

  • die Qual
  • Quatsch!



Pronouncing "sp" and "st"

These letters aren't difficult to pronounce exactly, but you do need to check and be careful with how you say them. Imagine there's an invisible 'shh' in "sp" and "st", so it becomes "shpuh" and "shtuh." The "shh" comes first, then the 'p' or 't.'

  • sprechen
  • verstehen

Compound words

There is one word guaranteed to make any German speaker’s blood run cold and have them immediately look at you in complete fear before fleeing the scene. Try it on your friends next time!



Rindfleischetikettierungsüberwachungsaufgabenübertragungsgesetz



This particularly infamous example is something called a compound word. These are essentially a bunch of separate words that are bunched up together to form a much longer word, but one that has a unique meaning depending on which words you join.

The above example is much less interesting in English (it’s the name of a law to do with labelling beef), but once you understand that all compound words are just little words that you can easily separate and translate or work on on their own, everything gets a little simpler.

You don’t even really have to worry about how to pronounce them: if you just treat it the way you would say a normal sentence, you’ll be easily understood! All you need to do is pronounce them as if they were separate.

It may help to draw lines in between the different words that make up a compound word so you can translate each part individually, then put it back together to work out what’s going on.
