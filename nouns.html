Nouns

Nouns in German work the same way they do in English – they are the names of people, places, concepts, things, and so on. The main difference is that German nouns have a gender, are always preceded by a preposition and the first letter is always capitalised.

Examples:

  • die Frau
  • der Mann
  • das Mädchen



NB: personal pronouns in German are not capitalised unless they are the first word at the beginning of a sentence – for example, ‘ich’ (I) will only be written as ‘Ich’ if it begins a sentence, such as ‘Ich will […]’ and so on. The same goes for the rest of them.

Noun prepositions and endings can also change depending on case.



Masculine nouns

The following words are mostly masculine.

Words with the following meaning:

  • Male humans and animals: der Vater, der Arzt, der Lehrer, der Hahn
  • Seasons, months, days of the week: der Sommer, der Mai, der Mittwoch
  • Wind and weather: der Wind, der Regen, der Hagel, der Sturm
  • Car brands: der Ford, der BMW, der Mercedes, der Toyota


Words with the following endings:

  • -ant: der Konsonant
  • -ast: der Kontrast
  • -ich: der Teppich
  • -ig: der Honig
  • -ismus: der Socialismus
  • -ling: der Liebling
  • -or: der Motor
  • -us: der Rythmus

Feminine nouns

The following words are mostly feminine.

Words with the following meanings:

  • Female humans and animals: die Mutter, die Ärztin, die Lehrerin, die Henne
  • Aeroplanes, motorbikes, ships: die Boeing, die BMW, die Suzuki, die 'Bismarck'
  • Rivers in Germany: die Donau, die Elbe, die Mosel, die Ruhr
  • Names of numerals: die Null, die Eins, die Hundert, die Million


Words with the following endings:

  • -a: die Pizza
  • -anz, -enz: die Eleganz
  • -ei: die Bücherei
  • -heit, -keit: die Krankheit
  • -ie: die Biologie
  • -in: die Freundin
  • -schaft: die Herrschaft
  • -sion, -tion: die Explosion
  • -tät: die Universität
  • -ung: die Bedeutung
  • -ur: die Natur


Nouns ending in -e are over 90% feminine. Most exceptions to this are masculine nouns for people, eg. der Junge.

Examples:

  • die Lampe
  • die Sahne
  • die Reise
  • die Bühne
  • die Tanne
  • die Diskette


Neuter nouns

The following words are mostly neuter.

Words with the following meanings:

  • Young people and animals: das Baby, das Kind, das Lamm
  • Continents, countries, towns: das alte Europa, das neue Polen, das geteilte Berlin
  • Metals, chemical elements, scientific units: das Gold, das Kobalt, das Ampère
  • Letters of the alphabet, musical notes: das A, das Ypsilon, das hohe C
  • Other parts of speech when used as nouns: das Du, das Gehen, das neue Deutsch


Words with the following endings:

  • -chen: das Mädchen
  • -lein: das Büchlein
  • -ma: das Drama
  • -ment: das Appartement
  • -tel: das Viertel
  • -tum: das Eigentum
  • -um: das Album


Nouns with the prefix Ge- are over 90% neuter. There is no consistent rule for exceptions, which isn't much help to anybody, but examples include der Gedanke and die Geschichte.

Examples:

  • das Gebäude
  • das Gesetz
  • das Gespräche
  • das Gewicht
  • das Gebot


Nouns ending in -nis are ~ 75% neuter. The rest are feminine, eg. die (Er)kenntnis, die Erlaubnis.

Examples:

  • das Bedürfnis
  • das Ereignis
  • das Erlebnis


Plurals

In English, we generally form plural nouns by just adding -s to the end of a word. German has no such simplicity, so it has seven ways of letting you know there's more than one of something present.

There's no easy or reliable way to tell which noun has which ending in the plural form, so the best thing to do is carry a dictionary with you at all times. Explain this in conversations once you fetch your hefty tome from your bag and skim through it in a conversation while your friends look at you in awe. Still, the one thing you can be sure of is that its preposition will always be 'die'.
