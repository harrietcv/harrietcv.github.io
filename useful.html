Useful phrases

Getting started

If you've just arrived in Germany (or any other German-speaking country or region) then you'll want to know a few things you'll be able to say there and then to help you get by. Perhaps you want to impress a German-speaking friend or relative with some quick phrases, or maybe you just simply want to survive as a tourist without floundering beyond the first thirty seconds. In a lot of the bigger German cities, people are (generally) fairly comfortable understanding and speaking English for tourists, but demonstrating your ability (and willingness) to try and learn their native language, even if it's only a few sentences, will go a long way towards making people feel comfortable around you.

The phrases here are listed in terms of formality as well as purpose. Very informal phrases are best kept for people you know very well and won't be insulted if you speak more casually with them, like family or good friends. Informal phrases are relatively casual, while formal phrases are your best bit if you're talking with strangers, such as asking for directions.

Greetings and farewells

Greetings

  • Hallo!

    This is an informal phrase.

    Meaning: "hello!"



  • Guten Morgen!

    Meaning: "good morning!"



  • Morgen!

    This is a very informal phrase.

    Meaning: "Morning!"



  • Guten Abend!

    Meaning: "good evening!"



  • Guten Tag!

    Meaning: "good day!" in general, but can also be used to say "good afternoon!"



  • Tag!

    This is a very informal phrase.

    Meaning: "Afternoon!" or "good day!" depending on context.



Farewells

  • Auf Wiedersehen!

    This is a formal phrase.

    Meaning: "goodbye!"



  • Tschüss!

    This is an informal phrase.

    Meaning: "bye!"



  • Tschüssie!

    This is a very informal phrase.

    Meaning: "byeeee!"



  • Bis bald / bis gleich!

    These are informal phrases.

    Meaning: "See you soon!"

Please and thank you

Please / you're welcome

  • Bitte!

    Meaning: This can mean both "please!" and "you're welcome!" depending on context. If you're asking somebody something, this would mean "please!", but if you get this in response then it would mean, "you're welcome!"

    It can also mean "tuck in!" or "help yourself!" if used in the context of someone offering you something (in this case food) because the stress is on the "please" - the request to take whatever it is is implied through context. German is a surprisingly flexible language!



  • Bitteschön!

    Meaning: "you're welcome!"



Thank you

  • Danke!

    This is an informal phrase.

    Meaning: "Thanks!"

    To make it more formal, you can simply say, "danke sehr!" (meaning, "Thanks very much! / Thanks a lot!")

    You can also make it slightly more formal by adding either "dir" or "Ihnen" - "danke dir" would be okay among friends and family, while "danke Ihnen" is formal enough for the workplace and dealing with strangers.



  • Dankeschön!

    This is a formal phrase.

    Meaning: "Thank you very much!"

Apologies

  • Entschuldigung!

    Meaning: "Excuse me!" or "I'm sorry!" depending on context. If you say "I'm sorry" this way, it is a much less formal (and emphatic) way of apologising, like for handing someone the wrong item, etc. You can also use it to start a request, same as English (eg: "Entschuldigung, können Sie mir sagen, wo das Hotel ist?")



  • Es tut mir leid!

    This is a formal phrase.

    Meaning: "I'm sorry!" but literally, "it makes me sad."

    You would use this phrase to show you are really sorry about something, or if it is a serious situation (such as expressing sympathy for someone in mourning).



  • [Es ist] kein problem!

    Meaning: "[It's] no problem!"

    Just like in English, omitting the "es ist" makes it more informal.

Chatting

  • Wie geht's?

    Meaning: "How are you?"

    To make it slightly more formal (but still informal enough for friends and family), you can say "wie geht es dir?"



  • Wie geht es Ihnen?

    This is a formal phrase.

    Meaning: "How are you?"



  • Es geht mir (sehr) gut/ (sehr) schlecht!

    Meaning: "I'm doing (really) well/ (really) badly!" or "I'm (really) good / (really) bad!"

    You can return the question in an informal way by asking, "und du?" ("and you?") or asking about someone else, such as "Wie geht Thomas?" ("How's Thomas? / How's Thomas doing?")



  • Woher kommen Sie?

    This is a formal phrase.

    Meaning: "Where do you come from?"



  • Woher kommst du?

    This is an informal phrase.

    Meaning: "Where do you come from?"



  • Ich komme aus...

    Meaning: I come from...

    • dem vereinigten Königreich or Großbritannien - "I come from the U.K.." Technically the second one means, "I come from Great Britain" if you want to split hairs, but that's up to you.
    • England - "I come from England."
    • Wales - "I come from Wales."
    • Schottland - "I come from Scotland."
    • (Nord)Irland - "I come from (Northern) Ireland."
    • Deutschland - "I come from Germany."
    • Österreich - "I come from Austria."
    • der Schweiz - "I come from Switzerland."
    • Frankreich - "I come from France."
    • Polen - "I come from Poland."
    • Italien - "I come from Italy."
    • dem Land der Eis und Schnee - "I come from the land of ice and snow."
    • Ich komme aus dem Land der Dichter und Denker - "I come from the land of poets and thinkers."

Requests

  • Wieviel kostet es/das?

    Meaning: "How much does it/that cost?"



  • Ich möchte...

    Meaning: "I would like [to have]..."



  • Ich möchte bezahlen [bitte]

    Meaning: "I'd like to pay [please] / I'd like the check/bill [please]"



  • Könnten Sie bitte mir helfen?

    This is a formal phrase.

    Meaning: "Can you please help me?"



  • Kannst du bitte mir helfen?

    This is an informal phrase.

    Meaning: "Can you help me?"

General

  • Ich verstehe (nicht)

    Meaning: "I don't understand"



  • Ich kann nicht verstehen, was du sagst

    This is an informal phrase.

    Meaning: "I can't understand what you're saying."



  • Ich könnte nicht verstehen, was Sie sagen.

    This is a formal phrase.

    Meaning: "I can't understand what you're saying."



  • Sprechen Sie Englisch?

    This is a formal phrase.

    Meaning: "Do you speak English?"



  • Sprichst du Englisch?

    This is an informal phrase.

    Meaning: "Do you speak English?"



  • Könnten Sie das bitte wiederholen?

    This is a formal phrase.

    Meaning: "Could you please repeat that?"



  • Kannst du das bitte widerholen?

    This is an informal phrase.

    Meaning: "Could you please repeat that?"



  • Könnten Sie das bitte noch einmal langsamer sagen?

    This is a formal phrase.

    Meaning: "Could you please say that again slower?"



  • Kannst du das bitte noch einmal langsamer sagen?

    This is an informal phrase.

    Meaning: "Could you please say that again slower?"



  • Mein Deutsch ist schlecht, tut mir leid

    This is an informal phrase.

    Meaning: "My German is bad, I'm sorry."



  • Ich spreche kein gutes Deutsch

    Meaning: "I don't speak good German / I don't speak German well"



  • Ich lerne (noch) Deutsch

    Meaning: "I'm (still) learning German"



  • Ich spreche noch nicht fließend Deutsch

    Meaning: "I don't speak fluent German yet."



  • Ich spreche fließend Deutsch

    Meaning: "I speak fluent German"



  • Ich kann keine Deutsch

    Meaning: "I don't know any German"



  • Ist es in Ordnung, wenn Sie mit mir Englisch sprechen bitte?

    This is a formal phrase.

    Meaning: "Is it alright if you please speak English with me?"



  • Ist es okay, wenn du Englisch mit mir sprichst bitte?

    This is an informal phrase.

    Meaning: "Is it alright if you please speak English with me?"



  • Deutschland ist ein wunderschönes Land!

    Meaning: "Germany is a wonderful country!"
